**Title:** LIMEN: a reviewer-safe public-source observatory for AI edge cases under source, language, and governance uncertainty

Dear Editors,

I am pleased to submit the above manuscript as a **Method Article** for consideration in F1000Research.

Public catalogues of "AI failures" are proliferating, and they are useful — but they almost universally place a final court ruling, a charging-stage allegation, a disclosed software vulnerability and a viral news anecdote in one table and count them together, inviting readers to misread a row count as prevalence. This manuscript contributes a reproducible method — an *evidence architecture* — that fixes this at the level of method rather than of any single dataset: it keeps source authority and evidence maturity explicit, never sums across denominator classes, and attaches to every case a claim ceiling stating what the record can and cannot be used to claim. The paper documents the data model, a refute-by-default adversarial verification protocol, the tiering criteria and the claim-ceiling schema, and demonstrates the method on a reviewed core of 250 evidence-grade cases (157 regulator/court records, 82 contested/interim matters, 11 security disclosures) plus a separately-fenced media layer of 46 incidents, drawn from 34 jurisdictions, with a technical-validation section reporting the adversarial verification results and the dataset's measured source-link coverage (233 of 250 evidence-grade cases, 93.2%, carry a direct link to the primary source).

I believe the work is well suited to F1000Research for three reasons. First, it is a methods-and-data contribution whose value is reproducibility and transparency rather than a novel result, which fits the Method Article format. Second, the underlying data are openly available (a browsable atlas and a downloadable JSON dataset; a citable archived snapshot with its own DOI will accompany the version under review), consistent with F1000Research's open-data requirements. Third, F1000Research's open, post-publication peer review is a natural fit for a paper whose entire thesis is that evidence should be inspectable and claims should be bounded in public.
