## Why this route is worth using now

LIMEN already has a publishable methods contribution even before stronger empirical closure in thin families. The contribution is not a claim to completeness or prevalence. It is a reproducible public-source observatory architecture that shows how source-family saturation, duplicate control, evidence-tier ceilings, zero-seed semantics, multilingual caution, and legal-conclusion limits materially change what is safe to count, visualize, and write.

## Intro framing

A workshop version should open by arguing that AI edge-case evidence is unusually prone to observability failure: important cases are split across regulator documents, court records, security notices, company statements, procurement traces, and multilingual local reporting. A flat incident list hides those differences. LIMEN's bounded contribution is to keep those differences visible enough that reviewers can tell what is strongly anchored, what is only provisionally countable, and what should remain limitations-only.

## Methods contribution

The workshop methods spine can be stated compactly as five controls:
1. declared source families instead of undifferentiated crawling;
2. duplicate-review quality control instead of silent row accumulation;
3. publication-safe evidence-tier and confidence-cap routing instead of raw record counts;
4. typed zero-seed semantics so gaps are not misread as absence;
5. multilingual and legal-caution routing so translation dependence and interpretive limits remain explicit.
